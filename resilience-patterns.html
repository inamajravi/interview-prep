Retry with Exponential Backoff

Trying again is fine — trying again immediately and repeatedly is how you cause an outage

Define: On a failed call, retry after a delay — and increase that delay exponentially with each subsequent failure (1s, 2s, 4s, 8s...), usually with jitter (random variation) added so many clients don't retry in perfect lockstep.

Analogy: If a shop's phone line is busy, calling back immediately and repeatedly (naive retry) just adds you to an ever-growing pile of callers all hammering the same busy line. Waiting a bit longer each time you get busy signal, and not calling at exactly the same second as everyone else doing the same thing (jitter), gives the line an actual chance to clear.
Why it exists — the failure mode without it

A dependency that's struggling under load gets retried immediately by every failed caller — which adds more load to an already-struggling system, making recovery less likely, not more. This is called a retry storm, and it's one of the most common ways a small blip becomes a full outage.

Trade-off
Retry safely whenThe operation is idempotent (same idempotency discipline as the Message Queues page) — retrying a duplicate GET or a properly idempotent POST is safe.
Don't blindly retry whenThe operation isn't idempotent, or the failure is a client error (4xx) rather than a transient one (5xx/timeout) — retrying a guaranteed-to-fail request just wastes calls.
Say this in interview

"Retry without backoff and jitter is how you turn a brief blip into an outage — every failed caller hammering the dependency at the same instant, repeatedly. I always pair retry with exponential backoff plus jitter, and I only retry operations I know are idempotent."

Circuit Breaker

Stop calling a dependency that's clearly down, instead of hammering it while it tries to recover

Define: Tracks failures to a dependency; once failures cross a threshold, the circuit "opens" — further calls fail immediately (fast) without even attempting the network call, for a cooldown period. After the cooldown, it goes "half-open," letting a small number of test calls through to check if the dependency has recovered before fully closing again.

Analogy: This is literally named after an electrical circuit breaker — when there's a fault (a short), the breaker trips and cuts power immediately, rather than letting current keep flowing into a damaged circuit and making things worse. You don't keep flipping the switch back on rapidly; you wait, then carefully test it once.
Why it exists, on top of retry

Retry handles brief, one-off blips well — but if a dependency is genuinely down for an extended period, retrying every single call (even with backoff) still means every caller keeps waiting out a full timeout on every request. A circuit breaker fails fast once it's confident the dependency is down, saving both the caller's latency and the dependency's recovery time.

Trade-off
Use whenCalling an external dependency that can genuinely be down for extended periods — protects both your own latency and gives the failing dependency room to recover.
Design carefullyThe open-circuit fallback behavior matters as much as the breaker itself — what do you return to the caller while open? A cached last-good value, a degraded response, or a clear error — silently failing is worse than the outage itself.
Say this in interview

"Retry and circuit breaker solve related but different problems — retry assumes the failure is brief and worth waiting out; circuit breaker assumes it might not be, and stops making things worse by failing fast. I'd use them together: retry with backoff for transient blips, wrapped by a circuit breaker for sustained outages."

Bulkhead

Isolate resource pools so one slow dependency can't starve everything else

Define: Named after a ship's bulkheads — physical compartments that stop one flooded section from sinking the entire vessel. In software: isolate resource pools (thread pools, connection pools) per dependency, so a slow or failing one can't exhaust resources needed by unrelated calls.

Analogy: A restaurant kitchen with one shared pool of chefs, where a single slow, complicated order (a struggling dependency) ties up every chef, leaving no one free to make the simple, fast orders that would otherwise go out in two minutes. Giving that complicated dish its own dedicated chef (a separate resource pool) means the rest of the kitchen keeps moving regardless of how long that one dish takes.
Why it exists — the failure mode without it

Without isolation, a single slow downstream call (all threads in a shared pool blocked waiting on it) can exhaust the entire thread pool — meaning completely unrelated, healthy requests to fast dependencies also start failing, purely because there are no threads left to serve them. This is how one degraded dependency cascades into a total outage of an otherwise-healthy service.

Trade-off
Use whenA service calls multiple downstream dependencies with meaningfully different reliability/latency profiles — isolate the risky ones.
CostMore resource pools to size and monitor — over-isolating everything adds operational overhead without proportional benefit for genuinely low-risk, fast dependencies.
Say this in interview

"The failure mode bulkheading prevents is resource starvation cascading — one slow dependency consuming every thread in a shared pool, taking down calls to completely unrelated, healthy dependencies. I'd isolate the pool specifically for whichever dependency has the worst reliability track record, not necessarily every single one."

Rate Limiting

Protecting yourself from too much traffic, not just protecting others from too little
Token Bucket (most common)

Define: A bucket holds tokens, refilled at a fixed rate. Each request consumes a token; if the bucket's empty, the request is rejected or queued. Naturally allows short bursts (using saved-up tokens) while enforcing a steady average rate over time.

Analogy: A theme park ride handing out a fixed number of boarding tokens per hour, refilled steadily — you can use a batch of saved tokens to bring a big group all at once (a burst), but you can't exceed the park's overall hourly throughput on average.
Why it exists

Protects a service from being overwhelmed (by a traffic spike, a buggy client in a retry loop, or a malicious actor) and enforces fair usage across multiple consumers of a shared API — the same instinct behind APIM/API Gateway throttling covered in the Azure and Kubernetes pages.

Trade-off
Token bucket whenBursty traffic is normal and acceptable, as long as the average stays bounded — most public APIs.
Fixed window / sliding window whenYou need simpler, more predictable enforcement and can accept less burst tolerance — easier to reason about but can allow a "double burst" right at a window boundary in the naive fixed-window version.
Say this in interview

"Token bucket is my default because real traffic is naturally bursty, and it's the only common approach that cleanly allows short bursts while still enforcing a real average rate — I'd only reach for a simpler fixed-window limiter when the traffic pattern genuinely doesn't need burst tolerance."

Timeouts

The simplest pattern here, and the one most often skipped entirely

Define: An explicit maximum wait time on any network call — after which you give up and treat it as a failure, rather than waiting indefinitely.

Why it's foundational

Without a timeout, a single hung dependency call can block a thread forever — and enough of those hung calls exhausts a thread pool exactly like the bulkhead failure mode described above, except with no dependency isolation even helping, because the pool itself never frees up. Every one of the other patterns on this page assumes a timeout exists somewhere underneath it — retry needs a bounded call to know when to retry; a circuit breaker needs bounded calls to count failures accurately.

Say this in interview

"Timeouts are the pattern everything else here is built on top of — without a bounded call, retry has nothing to retry against, and a circuit breaker can't reliably count failures. It's the simplest pattern on this page and also the one I see missing most often in real codebases."

Combining Patterns — the Real Interview Question

A senior answer names the layering, not just one pattern in isolation

These patterns aren't alternatives to each other — a well-protected call to an external dependency typically layers several: a timeout bounding each individual call attempt, wrapped in retry with exponential backoff for transient failures, wrapped in a circuit breaker that stops retrying entirely once it's confident the dependency is down, running inside a bulkhead-isolated resource pool so this one dependency can't starve unrelated calls, with a rate limiter protecting the calling service itself from being overwhelmed by its own upstream traffic.

Say this in interview

"I think of these as layers, not choices — timeout bounds each attempt, retry handles brief blips, circuit breaker handles sustained outages, bulkhead stops one dependency's problems from spreading to others, and rate limiting protects the service itself from its own inbound load. In .NET, Polly implements most of this directly as composable policies, which is exactly the layered structure I'd reach for rather than hand-rolling each one."

Rapid Recall Drill

What's a retry storm, and how do you prevent it?
Every failed caller retrying immediately, adding load to an already-struggling dependency — prevented with exponential backoff plus jitter.
How is a circuit breaker different from retry?
Retry assumes the failure is brief; circuit breaker assumes it might be sustained, and fails fast without even attempting the call once it's confident the dependency is down.
What failure mode does a bulkhead prevent?
One slow dependency exhausting a shared thread/connection pool, starving unrelated healthy calls of resources.
Why token bucket over a simple fixed-window rate limiter?
Naturally tolerates bursts while still enforcing an average rate — fixed window can allow a double-burst right at the window boundary.
Which pattern is everything else built on top of?
Timeouts — retry and circuit breakers both need a bounded call underneath them to function correctly.
